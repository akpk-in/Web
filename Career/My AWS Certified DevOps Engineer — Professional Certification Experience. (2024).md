## An Introduction To AWS Professional Level Certifications:

For those of you who may be starting your AWS Certification journey, professional-level certifications are notoriously difficult to achieve. I don’t say this to put anyone off but to motivate them to study for and pass the exam. From what I’ve heard from others, the AWS Professional level certifications are worth obtaining, unlike many certifications in the technology industry nowadays.

As AWS Professional level exams are notoriously difficult to pass, it ultimately means that it’s a sought-after certification to hold. In the uncertain times that we find ourselves in (the economic state of the world, Artificial Intelligence, etc), holding such a level certification can help you to land a great role should you find yourself in the position to need one, though holding a professional level certification would not be enough alone.

If you’re new to AWS/The AWS Certification path, I’d recommend checking out the AWS Certification’s landing page: [https://aws.amazon.com/certification/](https://aws.amazon.com/certification/) and the AWS Certification Path, which will give you an in-depth understanding as to which certification to book, study for and pass: [https://d1.awsstatic.com/training-and-certification/docs/AWS_certification_paths.pdf](https://d1.awsstatic.com/training-and-certification/docs/AWS_certification_paths.pdf)

## Prerequisites For The AWS Certified DevOps Engineer — Professional Exam:

Although there are no ‘hard’ perquisites before sitting the AWS Certified DevOps Engineer — Professional exam, I wouldn’t recommend just booking it and taking it without a well-thought approach (unless you’re an AWS Guru, then, you probably wouldn’t be reading this article anyway).

Before booking the AWS Certified DevOps Engineer — Professional exam, I’d recommend you have the following knowledge and/or experience:

- 3–5 years of hands-on experience with AWS. I’d love to break down which specific services, however, the exam covers SO MANY different services, and we’ll do so later on in the article.
- You’re super comfortable with the AWS Console in general.
- You understand the Software Development Lifecycle (SDLC).
- You have experience and/or knowledge of a programming language. Though this one isn’t a necessity, it’d certainly help with parts of the exam, such as understanding the Software Development Lifecycle (SDLC).
- You understand CI/CD (Continuous Integration, Continuous Delivery, AND Continuous Deployment — There are TWO ‘CDs’.
- You have experience with architecting and building infrastructure. I think experience here in general will help, even if it’s just on-premises infrastructure. Experience architecting and building infrastructure in AWS would be more beneficial though.
- You have experience with Infrastructure As Code. I didn’t have a whole lot of exposure to CloudFormation, however, I live and breathe Hashicorp Terraform, and the general knowledge of how IaC works would be of benefit to you.
- You have an understanding of running applications and the infrastructure they run on would be beneficial to you.
- You have an understanding of application and infrastructure monitoring, logging, and alerting.
- You have a LOT of time to study! Before sitting the exam, I took two courses which I’ll talk about later on in the article.

Before booking the AWS Certified DevOps Engineer — Professional exam, AWS recommends you have the following knowledge and/or experience:

- Experience developing code in at least one high-level programming language; building highly automated infrastructures; and administering operating systems.
- Understanding of modern development and operations processes and methodologies.
- Ability to implement and manage continuous delivery systems and methodologies on AWS.
- Ability to implement and automate security controls, governance processes, and compliance validation.
- Ability to define and deploy monitoring, metrics, and logging systems on AWS.

## AWS Certified DevOps Engineer — Professional Exam Day Tips:

Firstly, my first exam slot booking was a DISASTER! I decided that as my work laptop was already set up for the exam i.e. the Pearson VUE application, I’d use it to take the exam. This was a BAD choice! I won’t get into the nitty-gritty of it, however, in fairness to Pearson Vue, they fully recommend to NOT use your work laptop.

I ultimately got to answer the first question and then had to reschedule my exam, to say I was devastated was an understatement. Fast-forward a week or so later, I was able to re-book my exam.

The night before taking my exam, I couldn’t sleep. I spent some time scouring the internet for last-minute tips and tricks (which probably didn’t help me in reality). To save you from falling into the same rabbit hole that I did, here’s my tips:

- Get plenty of sleep! As simple of a tip as it is, I believe in this one. It’s personally a make-or-break tip for me!
- I can’t stress this one enough, GO TO THE BATHROOM! If you’re doing the exam remotely like me, 3 hours is a LONG time for those of us who have been burdened with ‘baby bladders’. You can’t leave your desk during the exam, so make sure you prepare yourself here.
- You get around two minutes per question, if you can’t answer a question quickly, then flag it and move on. Try not to get hung up on one particular question.
- If you take the ACloudGuru course, the exam tips are REALLY useful. Ultimately when utilising your knowledge along with these tips, you should have the ability to rule out some answers based on the services mentioned in them. To give an example, you’re not going to use AWS CodeBuild as your VCS tooling, you’d use AWS CodeCommit, this leads me to my next and final tip.
- Read the question thoroughly, then read the answers thoroughly. As goes the saying ‘the devil is in the detail’. In the exam, there are a few similar answers that will ultimately be used to catch you off guard, so make sure you’re on guard!
